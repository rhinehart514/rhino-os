# Solo Founder Pricing Guide

## The six rules

1. **Charge from day one.** Free users generate no signal. Even $1/mo changes behavior and proves demand. "I'll add pricing later" is avoidance, not strategy.

2. **One price, one tier.** Cognitive load is real. Don't force a choice between plans when you have 0 customers. Add tiers when you have data showing different user segments with different willingness-to-pay.

3. **Monthly until retention proven.** Annual plans hide churn. You need monthly data to know if people stay. Once 3-month retention exceeds 80%, offer annual at 2 months free.

4. **Price on value, not cost.** Your hosting costs $50/mo. That tells you nothing about what to charge. If you save someone 5 hours/week and their time is worth $100/hr, $50/mo is cheap and $200/mo is defensible.

5. **Round numbers.** $9, $19, $29, $49, $99. Not $17.43. Not $24.99. Clean numbers signal confidence. Penny pricing signals discount retail.

6. **No freemium before 100 paid users.** Free tiers cannibalize paid conversion at small scale. You can't learn from free users whether your product is worth paying for. Free comes after you've proven paid value.

## Stage-appropriate pricing

### Stage one (0 users)
- **Goal:** One person pays anything. The price validates demand.
- **Tactic:** Founding price (50% of target), locked forever for early adopters
- **Anti-pattern:** Building a pricing page before getting one payment

### Stage some (1-10 users)
- **Goal:** Find the price that retains, not the price that maximizes
- **Tactic:** Simple paid, one price. Track what correlates with retention.
- **Anti-pattern:** Adding tiers, enterprise pricing, volume discounts with 3 users

### Stage many (10-100 users)
- **Goal:** Unit economics that work. CAC < LTV with margin.
- **Tactic:** Two tiers (free limited + paid). Annual option.
- **Anti-pattern:** Three+ tiers, feature-gating that punishes power users

### Growth (100+ users)
- **Goal:** Revenue predictability and expansion revenue
- **Tactic:** Usage-based component, enterprise tier only if asked
- **Anti-pattern:** "Contact us" tier when nobody is contacting you

## Common pricing mistakes

### Anchoring to competitor price
Competitor charges $29/mo so you charge $19/mo. This positions you as the cheap alternative instead of the different one. Price based on your value, not their price.

### Price = features x rate
Adding up features and multiplying by some rate. Nobody pays for features. They pay for outcomes. One feature that saves 10 hours/week is worth more than 50 features that save 10 minutes total.

### "What would you pay?"
Asking users what they'd pay always under-values. Ask instead: "What is this worth to you?" or better, "What would you lose if this disappeared?"

### Discounting to close
Discounts train users to wait for discounts. If the price is wrong, change the price for everyone. If they can't afford it, they're not the right customer.

### Price changes every week
A/B testing pricing with 5 users is noise, not signal. Pick a price, run it for 30 days minimum, then evaluate retention, not signup rate. Conversion is vanity, retention is signal.
