# Positioning Frameworks

## Category Creation

When no existing category fits your product, create one. The goal is to make the listener understand what you do by defining a new space.

**Formula:** "[Familiar thing] for [specific audience]" or "[Familiar thing] that [key difference]"

**Examples:**
- "Cursor for Product Managers" (YC's framing)
- "A cofounder that measures" (rhino-os positioning)

**When to use:** When existing categories are saturated or misleading. When comparison to existing tools undervalues your product.

**Risk:** Nobody searches for a category that doesn't exist yet. Need content to define the space.

## Competitor Wedge

Position against a specific, well-known competitor. Define yourself by what they get wrong.

**Formula:** "[Competitor] but [key difference]" or "Unlike [competitor], we [difference]"

**Rules:**
- Only wedge against competitors your user already knows
- The difference must be observable in 30 seconds
- Never attack — contrast

**When to use:** When a dominant player exists and your user is frustrated with them.

## "Only For..." Framing

Narrow your audience explicitly. Counter-intuitive but effective: excluding people makes the included feel chosen.

**Formula:** "Built for [specific person]. Not for [everyone else]."

**Examples:**
- "For solo founders who ship. Not for teams with sprint planning."
- "For developers who want to know if their product got better. Not for developers who want clean code."

**When to use:** When your product genuinely serves a niche better than horizontal tools.

**Risk:** Limits TAM. That's the point — at stage one, TAM doesn't matter.

## Problem-Centric

Lead with the pain, not the solution. The reader recognizes the problem and reads on.

**Formula:** "[Problem statement]. [Product] does [solution]."

**Rules:**
- The problem must be something the reader says out loud (not something you tell them they have)
- Use their words, not yours (from customer-intel)

## Outcome-Centric

Lead with what's different after using the product. Skip the problem — go straight to the result.

**Formula:** "[Measurable outcome]. [How it works — one sentence]."

**Rules:**
- The outcome must be specific ("save time" = garbage, "know if your product got better in one command" = specific)
- Must be verifiable (the user can confirm it happened)
